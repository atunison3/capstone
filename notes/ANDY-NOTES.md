# Literature Review

## Voter Identification Laws and Turnout in the United States

This review article was the first in my research and was provided by a teammate. It introduced me to the issue of voter identification laws, specifically around the rhetorical problem(s) with them. It also generated thoughts to what type of data science analysis we should perform and what type of data we should look for (I am thinking a difference in difference analysis would be quite usefule here).

Highton, Benjamin. "Voter Identification Laws and Turnout in the United States". *Annual Reviews*. Vol 20, 2017, 149-167. [https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-polisci-051215-022822](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-polisci-051215-022822). Accessed June 13, 2026.

## A Meta-Analysis of Voter Mobilization Tactics by Electoral Salience

This article was another article provided by a teammate. While it affirmed my earlier idea of the different types of analysis to perform, the article also introduced the idea of election salience (though it did a horrible job of defining it). It introduced more problems associated with free elections and it referenced a book "Get Out the Vote" which looks intriguing and worth a read.

Mann, Christopher B. and Katherine Haenschen. "A Meta-Anslysis fo Voter Mobilization Tactics by Electoral Salience". *Science Direct*. Vol 87, 2024. [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379423001518](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379423001518). Accessed June 13, 2026.

## Get Out the Vote (Chapter One)

Get Out the Vote by Donald Green and Alan Gerber was referenced by Mann et al. While the introductory chapter was highly covariant with Mann's article, I did learn the difference of the strategys in voter mobilization vs voter persaussion. Our topic is on voter mobilization/turnout and it'll be important to distinguish between pursasive tactics and mobilization tactics.

Green, Donald P. and Alan S. Gerber. *Get Out the Vote*. The Brookings Institution 2015. Accessed online at [https://muse-jhu-edu.proxy.lib.umich.edu/book/42590](https://muse-jhu-edu.proxy.lib.umich.edu/book/42590) June 13, 2026.
